What Keeps Me Going

Getting to Know Myself

I’m getting older. That’s not always easy to admit—and it’s not always comfortable to feel. But with age has come something unexpected: clarity. I’ve learned what challenges me in the right way, what kind of effort reshapes me, and what kind of work helps me grow.

I’m drawn to hard problems—not because I enjoy difficulty for its own sake, but because that’s where change happens. When a project stretches me beyond my limits, I come out different: more focused, more curious, more grounded. That process isn’t always pleasant. But it’s what gives the work meaning. And it’s what keeps me going.

A Late Start

Today, I hold a Ph.D. and work as a professional researcher and data scientist. But that path wasn’t always clear. Until high school, I struggled in school—not because I didn’t like learning, but because I didn’t fit easily into the system. I didn’t get along well with classmates and found more connection and curiosity in online communities than in the classroom.

I learned on my own. Until middle school, I spent much of my time reading widely—across philosophy, literature, and popular science. I used to dream of becoming an animator. I was deeply inspired by the works of Hayao Miyazaki and Studio Ghibli—I watched their films over and over again. I also loved computers, and I still do.

What changed wasn’t me—it was the level of challenge. As the material became more demanding, I became more engaged. High school was better than middle school. College was better than high school. Graduate school pushed me in ways I’d never experienced before.

And it wasn’t easy. At my second-year review at UC Berkeley, a few faculty members gave me discouraging feedback. One professor wrote that I was unlikely to have a promising academic career because I didn’t write well.

That comment stayed with me—not just because it was harsh, but because it missed the context. English is my second language. I also speak Japanese and Chinese, but before starting graduate school, I had never lived in an English-speaking country. I came from a working-class family in South Korea. We didn’t have the resources for overseas study or specialized prep. I arrived in Berkeley with discipline, ideas, and a steep learning curve—not polish.

But I kept going—not to prove anyone wrong, but to prove to myself that I could express myself and find my voice. I studied how others wrote. I practiced. I rewrote and rethought every argument. And over time, writing became one of the many tools I could rely on—not just for research, but for connection. It’s not the only skill I bring to my work. I’ve always leaned on analytical and practical strengths, and I continue to build on them. But at that moment, the faculty didn’t see the full picture. They missed the potential behind the learning curve. I didn’t.

Every step of the way—from those early nights reading alone, to online communities that welcomed my curiosity, to classrooms and seminars that tested me—I learned, struggled, and grew. Graduate school was the hardest part. But it wasn’t the only part. Every step counted. And I’m still walking that path.

Research Is Personal

Hard work alone isn’t enough to sustain me. What keeps me going is meaning. I’m naturally curious, and sometimes I pursue ideas just for their own sake. But I’m most driven when the questions I’m asking matter to someone else—especially people navigating the kinds of barriers I’ve known firsthand.

I come from a working-class background. My father worked in a factory for more than 30 years. When he passed away from cancer, my mom had to carry the weight on her own. Financial stress shaped our daily life. In graduate school, I was fortunate to receive two fellowships—one from Berkeley and another from the Korea Foundation for Advanced Studies, one of the most competitive and prestigious scholarships for Korean students studying abroad. Even so, I had to take on side gigs to make ends meet. My wife couldn’t work due to visa restrictions. And we were living in the Bay Area, one of the most expensive regions in the United States.

Like many immigrants, I hustled. I studied, worked, and did everything I could to create stability for myself and my family. That experience doesn’t fade. It stays with me—in how I think about fairness, access, and what public institutions are for.

What I Want to Build

That’s why I became a social scientist. That’s why I conduct both basic and applied research. I want to take on difficult problems—but not in isolation. I want to help build systems that are more equitable, more responsive, and more humane.

I want to use research not just to describe the world, but to improve it.

The work I care about is hard—but it’s also good. It demands thought, humility, and persistence. But it points toward something better.

That’s what gets me out of bed in the morning. That’s what carried me through moments of failure and fatigue. That’s the thread that connects everything I’ve chosen to do—academically, professionally, personally.

And that’s what keeps me going.
